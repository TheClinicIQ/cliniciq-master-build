## Sections to Produce

### Section 1 — What Copy Does (The Single Job)

Copy has one job: move the right person from the state they are in to the state required to take the next action. Not inform. Not impress. Move. Every word either moves the reader forward or lets them slip away. This is the lens through which every section that follows is read.

Write this section as a full opening — not a bullet list. Establish the philosophy, the stakes, and why every framework in this document is a vehicle for that single job.

### Section 2 — The 11 DR Sequence Frameworks

The core of the document. For EACH framework write in full:
1. What it is
2. The psychological mechanism behind why it works
3. Exact structure (step by step)
4. When to use it — specific conditions under which it outperforms alternatives
5. A fully written health patient example (not described — actually written)
6. DISC profile weighting — which profiles respond best and why
7. Awareness level mapping — which of Schwartz's 5 levels it serves best

**The 11 frameworks:**

**1. Hook → Story → Offer (Brunson)**
The foundational narrative sequence. Hook earns attention. Story creates belief through the Epiphany Bridge. Offer is the only logical conclusion. The story is never just anecdote — it is the Epiphany Bridge: the expert's or patient's journey from the old vehicle to the discovery of the mechanism. Health application: the hook intercepts the patient's existing desire or pain; the story walks them through the discovery of the root cause mechanism (old vehicle = symptom suppression → wall → mechanism discovery → transformation); the offer is the implementation of everything just taught. When to use: webinars, VSLs, email sequences, long-form social. Not for short-form where the full arc can't play out.

**2. Hook → Value → CTA**
The premium short-form sequence. No story. The value delivery itself earns the CTA because it demonstrates real expertise. Value must be genuine and complete — not a tease. The CTA follows naturally from someone who has just received real insight. When to use: organic social content, short-form video (30–90 sec), email newsletters. Health application: teach a real mechanism insight → earn the webinar registration through demonstrated expertise, not through pressure.

**3. Problem → Agitate → Solve (PAS)**
Classical DR framework. Name the problem in precise avatar language. Agitate — deepen awareness of how bad it actually is, how it affects real daily life, what it is costing. Solve — introduce the solution. The agitation step is where most health copy falls flat. "Your fatigue is affecting your quality of life" is not agitation. "Your fatigue is costing you the ability to be present at your daughter's recital. It's the reason you close your office door at 2pm. It's what's behind every conversation your spouse has been trying not to have with you." That is agitation. When to use: short to medium-form copy, Problem Aware audiences.

**4. Problem → Agitate → Discredit → Solution (PADS)**
PAS with the critical dismantling step added. Name the solutions they've already tried. Validate them — these were reasonable attempts. Discredit them by revealing the mechanism they miss — not the person's failure to follow through, but a structural gap in what the approach can address. Then position the new vehicle as the one that reaches what the others couldn't. When to use: Solution Aware audiences who have already tried things and are skeptical. The most powerful sequence for moving jaded, battle-scarred health patients who say "I've tried everything."

**5. AIDA — Awareness → Interest → Desire → Action**
The original DR sequence. Create awareness of the problem or solution. Build interest through specificity and curiosity. Convert interest to desire through emotional connection to the transformation. Drive action. Maps directly to Schwartz's awareness levels — meet them at their current awareness and build through the stages. When to use: long-form sales pages, email sequences, webinar structures.

**6. Star → Story → Solution**
Lead with the character (Star — expert or patient). Tell the story in full narrative arc. Reveal the solution as the story's natural conclusion. The star is always the patient, not the expert. The patient is the hero. The expert is the guide who provided the mechanism. The story follows the Hero's Two Journeys simultaneously: the external journey (what they tried to do) and the internal journey (how they changed as a person). When to use: organic video, testimonial-style content, social proof content.

**7. Before → After → Bridge**
The simplest and most visual sequence. Before: current painful state in vivid, specific, experiential detail. After: the transformation — always in life terms, never in lab terms. Not "reduced inflammation markers" — "she got through a full day and was present for dinner without having to lie down first." Bridge: how to get from one to the other — the offer, the mechanism, the path. When to use: visual content, short-form ads, email subject line framing.

**8. The 4Ps — Promise → Picture → Proof → Push**
Make the Promise in New Opportunity language — never improvement language. Paint the Picture: sensory-specific future pacing of the outcome, relational and identity-level not just physical. Provide Proof: mechanism explanation + evidence + specific patient story. Push: the case for acting now — real urgency only, anchored to the compounding cost of another day of the underlying dysfunction progressing. When to use: sales pages, direct mail-style long-form email.

**9. The Fascination Framework (Bencivenga)**
Not a linear sequence but a modular system of curiosity-gap bullets. Each fascination creates an irresistible gap in understanding that can only be closed by engaging. Structure: "The [specific thing] that [benefit] — and [the counterintuitive element that makes it surprising]." When to use: bullets on sales pages, email PS lines, carousel slides, long-form ad copy. Health example: "The one thyroid marker your doctor almost certainly didn't test — and why it explains your fatigue more accurately than your TSH ever could." Each fascination must be specific, benefit-clear, and counterintuitive.

**10. The Open Loop Sequence**
Each piece of content opens a loop that is only closed by consuming the next. Every email, every video, every social post ends with a specific promise about what comes next — unresolved, compelling, impossible to ignore. When to use: email sequences, multi-part video series, social content series. Health application: every loop is mechanism-based. Each piece opens a "why" question — why does this keep happening, why hasn't the standard approach worked, why does this mechanism matter — that can only be answered by the next piece.

**11. DR Bullets → Close**
A concentrated burst of fascination bullets immediately before the CTA, when desire is at its peak. Names specific benefits, specific bonuses, specific fears addressed — each bullet adds to desire — then the CTA releases everything the bullets built. When to use: immediately before any CTA in any format, as the final acceleration before the ask.

For each framework the agent must write: the full psychological explanation of why it works, a fully written health patient example at the appropriate awareness level, the specific conditions where it outperforms alternatives, and the DISC + awareness level mapping.

### Section 3 — The Learning System: Framework Selection and Optimization Over Time

This section is what makes the platform intelligent rather than just competent. Document in full:

**The Decision Matrix**
A table mapping framework choice to: awareness level of audience, DISC profile weighting, format, traffic temperature (cold/warm/hot), and known performance characteristics. This is the starting state before performance data accumulates. Include the rationale for each mapping — why does PAS outperform Hook→Story→Offer for cold traffic Problem Aware audiences? Why does Hook→Value→CTA dominate organic social for D and I profiles? Each mapping should be explained, not just listed.

**Performance Tagging Standard**
Every asset produced by any Builder agent is tagged at creation with: framework used, awareness level targeted, DISC weight applied, platform, traffic temperature, and expert niche. Without tagging, data accumulates but can't be learned from. Document the exact tagging schema and how it integrates with the `hook_performance_data.md` file.

**The Optimization Rule**
When a framework combination accumulates sufficient data points and underperforms the platform baseline by a defined threshold, it is flagged for testing an alternative. When it outperforms, its weight in the decision matrix increases. The Learning Loop Agent (Layer 4) manages actual optimization — but the logic for what to track, how to calculate baselines, when to promote or demote, and what "sufficient data" means at different traffic volume tiers lives here.

**The "Never Retire, Only Demote" Principle**
No framework is permanently eliminated. Some are seasonal. Some are audience-segment specific. Some respond to cultural moments. A framework that underperforms in cold traffic for hormone health audiences in Q1 may be the strongest performer in warm retargeting for gut health audiences in Q4. Reduced weight, never removed. The platform maintains diversity in its testing portfolio.

**The Test-Rotation Standard**
Even when a framework combination is performing well, a defined percentage of budget (document the recommended %) goes to testing alternative frameworks. The platform never becomes so dependent on one sequence that when performance degrades (as all creative does over time), there is no proven alternative ready. Document how this rotation is managed by the Monthly Master Plan and the Ad Performance Analyst.

### Section 4 — The 5 Levels of Awareness: What the Master Copywriter Does at Each Level

Written as positive craft intelligence — what a masterful copywriter thinks, feels, inhabits, and does at each level. Not restrictions. The actual cognitive and creative process.

For each level write a full section covering: the copywriter's job, the question they inhabit, the craft challenge, a fully written health example, and the test for whether it worked.

**Level 1 — Unaware**
The copywriter's job is to hold up a mirror so precise and specific that the person feels seen before they know who is speaking or what is being offered. No solution. No condition name. No category. Just a human experience, named precisely enough that the exact right person stops and says "yes — that's exactly what my 2pm feels like." The question the copywriter inhabits: what is this person experiencing in their daily life that they have not yet connected to anything larger? The writing describes the specific texture of their daily reality — the moment, the feeling, the small accommodation they've made without ever naming it as a problem. The craft challenge: write about a problem without ever calling it a problem.

**Level 2 — Problem Aware**
The copywriter's job is deep resonance — going further emotionally than the person has allowed themselves to go consciously. The question: what has this person not yet heard that would make them feel completely understood for the first time? Not the surface pain — the layer underneath it. The real cost. The grief, not just the symptom. The fear, not just the complaint. The copy names what the person carries in private — the 2am fear, the grief for who they used to be, the shame of not having figured this out yet — before naming it as something addressable. The craft challenge: name the pain at its deepest level without going so deep the person needs to protect themselves by disengaging.

**Level 3 — Solution Aware**
The copywriter's job is productive disruption — cognitive dissonance that is exciting rather than threatening. This person thinks they understand the solution landscape. They may feel they're already doing something. The question: what specific mechanism insight would make this person realize their current approach addresses the wrong thing? The copy doesn't attack what they're doing — it reveals why the mechanism they're using can't reach the root cause. "I never thought of it that way" is the goal, not "you're calling me wrong." The craft challenge: create genuine cognitive disruption in a sophisticated, health-aware person without triggering defensiveness.

**Level 4 — Product Aware**
The copywriter's job is conviction and removal of the final, specific obstacle. Not more education — this person has enough. Something specific is holding them back. The question: what is the one thing between this person and the decision, and how do I address it directly without flinching? Tools: case-matched testimonials (someone with their exact fear who succeeded anyway), a guarantee bold enough to genuinely remove risk, urgency anchored to the compounding cost of another day of the underlying dysfunction progressing. The craft challenge: be direct without being pushy. Create urgency without manufacturing it. Match proof to the exact objection, not to a general one.

**Level 5 — Most Aware**
The copywriter's job is activation — nothing else. This person has decided. They're circling. The question: what is the honest, real cost of another day of inaction, and can it be said in a way that lands as clarity rather than manipulation? No story needed. No mechanism. No proof. Just the truth, said directly: every day this compounds, and the moments lost don't come back. The craft challenge: create urgency that feels like a caring friend telling them something true — not pressure, not a countdown timer — just the honest cost of waiting one more day.

### Section 5 — The Perfect Webinar (VSL): Complete and Precise

The agent has read `expert_secrets_full_synthesis.md` and `step13b_webinar_builder_deep_spec.md`. This section documents the complete copy architecture of the Perfect Webinar as the VSL standard for all long-form health video content.

**The One Big Domino**
One belief. Perry Belcher's data: every additional belief required halves conversion. The Big Domino statement formula. How every section of the VSL points back to it. How to construct it before writing a single word of script.

**The Introduction — Five Jobs:**
1. Authority without arrogance — results first, credentials as evidence
2. Commitment hook — future pacing as a reason to stay, not a request
3. Pre-handle the top vehicle objection — name it, validate it, open the door
4. Genuine hope — specific possibility for someone in their exact situation
5. Open loops — specific promises with timestamp-level detail

**Trial Closes — All 16, with Health Applications:**
Document all 16 trial closes from Expert Secrets. For each: the mechanic, why it works psychologically, a fully written health patient application. Frequency: every 60 seconds throughout the entire webinar. Not clustered at the close — woven into every section from minute one. Include Brunson's test data: adding trial closes to an existing automated webinar moved revenue per registrant from $9.45 to $16.50. No other change.

**The Three Secrets — The 5-Step Pattern:**
Step 1: Name the false belief in exact avatar language
Step 2: Epiphany Bridge story — the "aha" comes from the story before the teaching
Step 3: The teaching — mechanism specific enough that the avatar understands why the old approach couldn't reach it
Step 4: State the new belief explicitly — never leave them to conclude it themselves
Step 5: Proof — one patient story, named or aliased, specific condition, specific previous attempts, specific protocol, specific result with timeframe

**The Epiphany Bridge — 8 Sections:**
For each section: what it must accomplish and why shortcutting it breaks the belief transfer. Backstory → Desires (external + internal both stated) → The Wall → The Epiphany → The Plan → The Conflict → The Achievement → The Transformation.

**The Three False Beliefs Applied to Health:**
Secret 1 (Vehicle): old vehicle = symptom suppression. New vehicle = root cause medicine. The mechanism must be named specifically, explained in plain language, supported by evidence, and categorically different — if the avatar can file it under anything they've already tried and rejected, the sale is over.
Secret 2 (Internal): tool failure vs. compliance failure. The ideal patient story features someone who explicitly gave up and then succeeded — not through discipline but through the right mechanism.
Secret 3 (External): cost, access, and time — the three most common external objections in health. Each addressed specifically. Acknowledge as reasonable given the old vehicle. Show the new vehicle doesn't require what they think it requires.

**The Stack and Close — Complete Sequence:**
Every element in order with the psychological reasoning for why it is in this exact position and what breaks if it is skipped or reordered:
1. Transition to offer
2. Stack build element by element (introduce → add to stack — never a flat list; this repetition produces 300% more conversion than flat presentation)
3. If/All statements (three, one per Secret)
4. "I Had Two Choices"
5. "What Would the End Result Be Worth?"
6. Price reveal (CTA link appears and stays on every subsequent slide)
7. Price justification
8. "You've Got Two Choices"
9. The guarantee (specific, confident, health-compliant)
10. "The Real Question Is This"
11. Final stack slide + real urgency only
12. CTA + Q&A slide

Full (75–90 min) vs. Short (45–60 min): specific compression rules per phase — what can compress and what never compresses.

### Section 7 — Platform-Specific Copy Calibrations

For each format, the positive craft intelligence — what the master copywriter does here, the specific decisions they make, the standards they hold.

**Meta Ad Primary Text**
Three variant types and their distinct conversion jobs: pain-led (opens with the avatar's specific experience, narrows fast to the solution), curiosity-led (opens with the counterintuitive mechanism claim, builds intrigue, drives the click), proof-led (opens with a specific patient result, immediately credible, builds belief). Headline types and when to use each. Mobile-first writing standard — the first line must stand alone in collapsed view. Health compliance integration — how to write powerfully within policy.

**Email**
Subject line as hook. Preview text as subhook — extends the subject line without repeating it. First line standard: never start with "I," always start with the reader's world. PS line usage (second most-read element in any email — CTA, proof point, or open loop). CTA placement at 60–70% through the email — after value is delivered, before energy drops. One CTA per email. The "would a friend send this?" test for health email.

**SMS**
160 characters. First name. One hook, one link. The click is the only job. What SMS is used for (follow-up, appointment confirmation, re-engagement) and what it is never used for (long-form selling, multi-step sequences).

**Landing Page**
Above-fold standard: hook headline + subheadline + one immediate proof element — all visible before scroll. The mechanism section as the second most important element on the page. Proof, stack, guarantee, FAQ — in that order. FAQ as final objection-handling pass — every question is a real objection the avatar carries.

**Short-Form Video (30–90 sec)**
Hook in frames 0–3: one dominant element, spoken and visual hooks aligned. The 10-second re-hook rule — something new every 10 seconds or viewers leave. CTA assumes persuasion through delivered value, not sales pitch language.

**Organic Social Caption**
Hook line as the first line (the only line visible before "more"). Body delivers genuine value — a complete thought, not a tease. CTA is the natural next step for someone who received real value. The share test: would someone who will never buy still share this because it's genuinely useful?

**Carousel**
Slide 1 = hook only. Nothing else. Slides 2–4 = proof, mechanism, or benefit bullets. Final slide = single CTA. Visual thread across all slides. When carousel outperforms single image.

### Section 8 — NLP Application by Copy Format

For each NLP tool: the mechanics, why it works psychologically, a fully written health patient example, and the awareness level + format where it lands hardest.

**Presuppositions** — Language that assumes the desired outcome is already happening. "When you finally get your energy back..." not "If you want more energy..." Applied throughout hooks, VSL introduction, email subject lines. The presupposition bypasses the "will this work?" evaluation and starts the reader inside the outcome.

**Embedded Commands** — Instructions embedded within larger sentences so they bypass conscious resistance. "Go ahead and imagine what it would feel like..." Applied in CTAs, future pacing sections, demonstration moments.

**Pacing and Leading** — Match the avatar's current reality before leading them toward a new one. Three paces minimum before the first lead. Pace too little and the lead feels presumptuous. Applied throughout agitation sections, every webinar introduction, every email opening.

**Future Pacing** — Putting the person vividly inside the outcome before they've committed. The decision becomes about protecting something they've already glimpsed. Applied in webinar introduction, "What Would the End Result Be Worth?" close, desire sections of any format.

**Reframing** — Changing the meaning of an experience without dismissing it. "You tried those things and they didn't work — which actually proves you're someone who takes your health seriously, not someone who gives up easily." Applied in Secret 2 internal belief section, Past Struggles section, any sequence addressing failed past attempts.

**Sensory-Specific Language** — Language that activates internal experience rather than describing it abstractly. Not "you'll feel better" — "you'll wake up and your first thought won't be about how tired you already are." The more sensory-specific, the more the reader experiences the outcome as real.

**Curiosity Gaps** — The known-unknown gap the brain cannot leave open. Specific enough to be real, open enough to be irresistible. Applied in hooks, fascination bullets, email subject lines, open loop sequences.

**Pattern Interrupts** — Breaking the expected flow to recapture attention. Applied at hook level, at re-hook points every 10 seconds in video, in email subject lines, at key transitions in long-form copy.
