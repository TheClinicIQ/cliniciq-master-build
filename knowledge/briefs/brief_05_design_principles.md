## Sections to Produce

### Section 1 — The Brand-Direct Design Philosophy

Define the Brand-Direct aesthetic fully. Document both extremes and why each fails in health:

**Pure DR visual language — why it fails in health:**
Aggressive orange buttons, fake countdown graphics, cluttered layouts with 12 competing elements, fluorescent borders, "CLICK HERE NOW" aesthetics. Why this approach works in some markets but fails in health: the health patient is often scared, often burned before by products and programs, and reads visual desperation as a signal of low quality and high manipulation risk. Trust is the prerequisite to conversion in health. Aggressive DR design destroys trust before the copy even gets a chance.

**Pure brand visual language — why it fails in health conversion:**
Minimalist to the point of no urgency. White space without direction. Aspirational imagery with no clear offer. Beautiful, passive content that asks for nothing. Why this builds awareness but bleeds conversion: health patients who need to take action don't take action from passive beauty alone. They need clarity about the path forward.

**The Brand-Direct middle:**
Premium execution with clear conversion intent. The specific visual decisions that create this balance:
- Color: premium palette with one high-contrast conversion color used purposefully
- Typography: clean hierarchy that guides the eye to the message first, the CTA second
- Imagery: real and warm, not stock and sterile
- Layout: generous space where trust is being built, focused density where action is requested
- White space as a trust signal, not as timidity or avoidance

### Section 2 — Visual Hierarchy System

**The 3-second scan rule:** every creative asset is evaluated in 3 seconds. What is seen in those seconds, and in what order, determines whether the person stays or leaves.

**The 3-element hierarchy for every asset:**
1. Primary element — the hook (text or face or image) — what stops the scroll
2. Secondary element — the supporting claim or mechanism — what earns the stay
3. Tertiary element — the CTA or brand indicator — what captures the action

**Eye movement patterns by format:**

Z-pattern: how the eye moves on desktop-width horizontal layouts. Top-left → top-right diagonal → bottom-left → bottom-right. When to use Z-pattern: landing pages, wide-format ads, desktop email.

F-pattern: how the eye moves when scanning text-heavy content. Two horizontal passes across the top, then a vertical scan down the left edge. When to use F-pattern awareness: long-form landing pages, email body. Key content must appear in the top two horizontal passes.

Gravity-center: how the eye moves on mobile — dominant center element first, then radiates outward. Why mobile layouts center the primary element: on a 390px screen held vertically, the center of the screen is the natural focal point.

**Hierarchy failure modes — each with what it looks like, why it fails, and the correct alternative:**
- Two elements of equal visual weight competing (the eye gives up and scrolls past)
- CTA positioned before sufficient value is established (the eye skips past before trust is built)
- Brand logo as the dominant element (irrelevant until the person already trusts you)
- Too many elements at too similar sizes (everything important = nothing important)
- Text that is visually subordinate to the background image (the copy is invisible)

### Section 3 — Color Psychology for Health Audiences

Specific to health patient psychology — not generic color theory.

**The three color functions:**

Trust colors: create credibility and safety before the person has read a word. Health-specific trust colors: deep forest green (nature, healing, life, growth), deep navy blue (expertise, reliability, calm authority), muted gold or amber (warmth, premium, established credibility). Why these work: the health patient needs to feel safe before they will consider spending money on their health. Trust colors lower the guard.

Urgency colors: create movement toward action without triggering the "this is manipulation" alarm. Health-appropriate urgency: warm red used sparingly (a single accent element, never dominant), deep amber or terracotta (warmth + movement, less threatening than pure red). Why neon urgency fails in health: electric yellow, hot pink, neon orange signal the kind of marketing this audience has learned to distrust.

Credibility colors: create a frame of professionalism that makes the trust and urgency colors more believable. White space (its absence is a design decision — it signals confidence and organization), clean neutral backgrounds (light gray, warm white, off-white), dark text on light backgrounds for readability.

**Colors to avoid in health creative:**
- Hospital blue and clinical white — triggers the medical system association, which health patients seeking a different path are specifically trying to move away from
- Aggressive neon in any form — signals cheap and manipulative
- Pure black as the dominant background — too heavy for a health context where warmth, hope, and transformation are the emotional goals

**Adapting to expert brand colors:**
Every expert has their own palette. Rules for maintaining Brand-Direct principles when the expert's brand is outside the recommended palette: use brand colors for identity elements (logo, name, accent), but always maintain conversion color contrast. Never let a brand color substitute for the high-contrast CTA color if it doesn't meet minimum contrast requirements. The brand serves the conversion, not the reverse.

**Contrast requirements:**
- CTA button: minimum 4.5:1 contrast ratio against background (WCAG AA)
- Body text: minimum 4.5:1
- Large display text on images: minimum 3:1, prefer 4.5:1
- Text over images: requires shadow, outline, or overlay because image backgrounds are not controlled solid colors

### Section 4 — Typography for Conversion

**Hook text on images:**
- Maximum 5 words — anything longer loses stopping power
- Maximum weight available in the typeface (bold, heavy, or black weight)
- Minimum 85% contrast ratio against background (requires treatment if on a complex image)
- Size: large enough to read instantly at thumbnail size (50% scale)
- Never script or decorative fonts for hook text — readability at speed is the only criterion

**Supporting text (secondary claim):**
- Maximum 12 words
- Medium weight — clearly subordinate to the hook but clearly readable
- Sufficient size separation from hook so the hierarchy is immediately obvious
- Color: slightly lower contrast than hook to establish visual subordination

**CTA text:**
- Specific action word + outcome or urgency
- "Start Your Protocol" not "Learn More"
- "Book Your Assessment" not "Click Here"
- "Get the Free Guide" not "Download"
- Never all-caps in full sentences (shouts, reduces comprehension speed)
- Consistent pill or button shape across all assets

**Font personality guide for health:**
- Authority/expertise: serif fonts (Georgia, Playfair Display, Cormorant) — for expert name treatments and credentials
- Clean/trustworthy: humanist sans-serif (Inter, Nunito, Poppins, Lato) — for body, supporting text, UI
- Energy/movement: geometric sans (Montserrat, Futura) — sparingly for hooks and headlines
- Never use: Comic Sans, ultra-thin weights at small sizes, decorative display fonts that prioritize aesthetic over readability

**The 2-font rule:** Maximum two typefaces per creative system. One for headlines/hooks, one for body/supporting. Exception: expert's brand may introduce a third for name treatment only.

### Section 5 — Imagery Standards

**What converts in health creative:**

Real people in real moments. The difference from stock: stock health photography shows everyone already having the result. Real health creative shows the authentic journey — which is what the avatar is in. They don't connect with the polished after yet. They connect with the recognizable moment of someone who found a way through.

Transformation-adjacent imagery: aspiration without explicit before/after (policy-compliant). Show the transformation through life moments:
- Energy shown as someone running with their kids in the afternoon, not a before/after weight comparison
- Mental clarity shown as someone at their desk, fully engaged and upright, coffee untouched
- Recovery shown as someone present in a conversation, not distracted by their body

Clinical-adjacent without clinical: warmth and expertise simultaneously. The expert in a natural setting with visible care. Lab environments that feel curious and clean, not sterile and intimidating. Protocol imagery that looks serious and premium, not pharmaceutical.

Nature and vitality: specific over generic. A specific morning trail in filtered light, not "nature." Colorful whole vegetables being prepared, not "healthy eating."

**The "would a real patient share this?" test:** Apply before using any image. Stock photography of models in hospital gowns fails immediately. A genuine-feeling photo of a woman with visible energy and engagement passes. If a health patient would share the image because it reflects something true about their journey — it's right. If it looks purchased for advertising — reconsider.

**What kills conversion in health imagery:**
- Obvious stock photography (recognizable poses, lighting, and "diversity group" compositions)
- Hospital imagery unless absolutely necessary — triggers fear and the medical system the patient may be trying to move away from
- Sad, suffering imagery — creates connection at awareness Level 1–2 but damages conversion at warmer stages and feels exploitative
- Generic supplement bottle photography — feels low-quality unless the supplement itself is the premium hero
- Explicit before/after imagery — policy violation and creates unrealistic expectations that increase refunds

### Section 6 — Video Visual Standards

**The hook frame (0:00–0:03): inviolable for every video format.**

Rules:
- One dominant visual element — face OR bold text OR high-contrast scene. Never two competing elements.
- Spoken hook and visual hook aligned — what is said and what is shown reinforce the same message
- Hook text appears on screen within the first 5 frames, not after a delay
- No intro animations, logo reveals, or music fade-ins without a simultaneous visual hook
- Color contrast in frame 1: dominant element must stand out from background

**Pacing rules by format:**
- Short-form (15–60 sec): cut every 2–3 seconds
- Medium-form (60–300 sec): cut every 8–15 seconds
- VSL/Long-form (15+ min): cut every 20–30 seconds with b-roll
- 10-second re-hook rule for any format: something new (new idea, new question, new pattern interrupt) every 10 seconds or viewers leave

**B-roll selection:**
- Specific over generic always
- Movement preferred over static
- Emotional resonance: b-roll evokes the emotional state of the after, not the clinical state of the process
- Authenticity: if it could be from a real patient's life or a real clinical interaction, it's right. If it looks produced, it hurts trust.

**Text overlay:**
- Maximum 6 words per text card
- Center-frame safe zone only (not in top 14% or bottom 20% which are UI zones)
- Appears on the word it emphasizes, not before
- Contrast treatment: outline or drop shadow over any complex background
- Duration: on screen maximum 3–4 seconds (eye reads in 2 seconds; longer creates lag)

### Section 7 — Carousel Design System

**When carousel outperforms single image:**
- Multiple mechanisms or proof points to show sequentially
- A process or transformation that benefits from visual progression
- Target audience is engaged and warm (carousels require commitment)
- Content is genuinely serialized — each slide depends on the previous

**The carousel sequence:**
- Slide 1: hook only. Nothing else. One powerful hook that makes swiping the obvious next action.
- Slides 2–4: mechanism, proof, or benefit bullets. One idea per slide. Short copy. Strong visual.
- Final slide: single CTA only. Clear, direct, specific.

**Visual thread:** One consistent design element running across all slides to create continuity — a color bar at the same position, consistent icon treatment, repeating background pattern. Signals that the slides belong together and rewards completion.

**Slide count:** 4–7 optimal. Under 4: insufficient depth to justify carousel format. Over 7: commitment too high for cold traffic.

### Section 8 — The Compliance-Design Intersection

**Aspiration vs. pain imagery:**
- Pain imagery: used sparingly, only at Levels 1–2, framed as recognition not exploitation. Shows the experience, not the person as a victim. Never gratuitous.
- Aspiration imagery: the primary conversion tool. Shows what the avatar wants to feel, be, and have — not what they don't have. Identity, relationships, energy, joy — not just weight or symptoms.

**The implicit transformation:**
Show transformation through contrast of context, not contrast of body or symptoms.
- Before image: tired woman at desk, low-energy posture, untouched coffee
- After image: same woman type, same desk, upright, engaged, focused
- The transformation shown in posture, energy, engagement — not in physical appearance comparison

**Image/text combination test:**
Two compliant elements can combine to create a non-compliant implication. "Transform your health" as copy + an obvious before/after visual = implied before/after comparison even though neither element alone is a violation. Always consider what the combination implies, not just what each element says individually.

### Section 9 — Platform-Specific Visual Adjustments

**Meta Feed (Facebook + Instagram):** Clean, premium, strong hierarchy. Either high-quality photography or bold text on brand color. The "native-looking" principle — the best converting feed ads look like they belong in the feed, not like they're interrupting it.

**Instagram Stories and Reels:** Vertical canvas. Text in center third only. Movement in the first frame. The UGC aesthetic performs — slightly imperfect, direct-to-camera, phone-quality video outperforms polished production in this format.

**YouTube Thumbnail:** Bold expressive face showing the relevant emotion + large readable text (4–5 words) + high contrast background. The face and text tell the story together. Test: does the thumbnail alone create enough curiosity to earn the click?

**TikTok:** Lower production value outperforms polished in this format. Expert in a natural personal space (home office, kitchen, clinic) with natural light. Lower production quality signals authenticity.

### Section 10 — Do/Don't Reference Table

A rapid-reference table of the most important design decisions across health creative. Minimum 25 rows.

| Context | Do | Don't | Why |
|---------|-----|-------|-----|
| Hook text on image | 5 words max, maximum weight | Long sentences in regular weight | Stops the scroll; readable at thumbnail size |
| CTA button | Specific action + outcome | "Learn More" / "Click Here" | Specificity signals what happens next |
| Imagery for energy content | Real person running with kids in afternoon | Stock photo of woman jogging on white background | Real imagery builds trust; stock imagery signals advertising |
| Color for urgency | Warm red as single accent element | Neon orange or fluorescent dominant color | Neon signals manipulation to health audience |
| Before/after | Show context contrast (posture, engagement) | Show body comparison | Policy compliance + avoids unrealistic expectations |
| Font on image | Bold/heavy weight only | Thin or light weight | Illegible at reduced size in feed |
| Background on landing page | Clean neutral (warm white, light gray) | Pure white hospital aesthetic | Warm neutrals feel human; clinical white triggers medical fear |
| Expert positioning photo | Natural setting, genuine engagement | Formal portrait, white coat, posed | Relatability > credentials for health audience |
| Number of visual elements | One dominant, one supporting, one CTA | 5+ elements competing | Hierarchy requires one thing to win |
| Text over complex imagery | Text on color band or with outline | Text floating over detailed background | Contrast and readability above all |
| Video opening frame | Dominant visual + emotion + text overlay | Logo animation or generic intro | First frame determines scroll decision |
| Carousel slide 1 | Hook only | Hook + proof + CTA on first slide | Earn the swipe before delivering value |
| Testimonial imagery | Real person photo with genuine expression | Stock model representing "satisfied customer" | Authenticity signals truth |
| Price display on landing page | Bold, centered, prominent after full value stack | Hidden or minimized price | Confidence in price is a trust signal |
| Guarantee visual | Clear shield, checkmark, or handshake visual | No visual — just text | Visual reinforces the trust signal |
| Section spacing (landing page) | 80px between sections desktop | Cramped sections with 20px gaps | Generous spacing signals confidence and premium quality |
| Mobile hero section | CTA visible without scroll on 390px viewport | CTA hidden below fold on mobile | Mobile CTA visibility is the #1 landing page conversion lever |
| Social proof placement | Adjacent to the first CTA | Only at the bottom of the page | Proof needed at the moment of decision |
| Color palette | 3 colors maximum: trust + credibility + urgency | 7+ brand colors applied equally | Too many colors creates visual noise and dilutes impact |
| Video pacing (short-form) | Cut every 2–3 seconds | Static talking head for 30 seconds | Attention captured must be maintained with visual change |
| Image file format | WebP for landing pages, JPEG for photos, PNG for graphics | JPEG for logos (artifacts), GIF for landing pages | Right format for each use case |
| Expert name treatment | Serif font, medium size, subordinate to hook | Expert name larger than the hook | The hook earns attention; the expert earns trust after |
| Mechanism visual | Diagram or process illustration | Clip art or generic stock infographic | Specific visual signals real intelligence |
| Pain imagery | Recognition framing ("does this describe you?") | Suffering framing (gratuitous pain imagery) | Recognition creates connection; exploitation creates recoil |
| UGC-style video | Natural setting, direct-to-camera, slight imperfection | Professional three-point lighting in UGC format | The imperfection is the authenticity signal |

### Section 11 — The Creative Freedom Directive

These principles are the floor. The most powerful health creative ever produced followed these rules and then found the specific image, the specific typographic treatment, the specific color combination that had never been seen before in quite that way — and stopped the scroll cold.

The principles prevent bad work. They do not produce great work. Great work requires genuine creative intelligence applied through them. That is the standard every clinicIQ Visual Production Agent holds itself to: not "this is compliant" or "this follows the design principles" but "this is genuinely excellent and will earn the attention of the person I'm trying to reach."
