## Purpose

The offer is the commercial engine of the entire practice. Every funnel, every ad, every webinar, every email sequence, every sales script — all of it exists to move a specific person toward a specific offer at a specific price. If the offer is weak, the best copy in the world cannot save it. If the offer is strong, even mediocre copy converts.

This step defines how the Onboarding Agent extracts, builds, and structures the complete offer architecture for the health expert — using the Hormozi, Brunson, and Fladlein frameworks as the underlying architecture — and how that offer architecture flows into every downstream output.

The agent's job here is not just to capture what the expert currently sells. It is to help them build what they should be selling — using the clinicIQ 4-tier stack model as the standard and filling gaps where the expert's current offer structure is missing tiers, underpriced, or poorly positioned.

## The clinicIQ 4-Tier Offer Stack Model

Every health expert needs four offers, not one. Most have one or two. The agent's job is to identify which tiers exist and build the missing ones.

### Tier 1 — Attraction Offer (Free or Low-Cost Entry)
**Purpose:** Get the right person to raise their hand. Creates the lead. Filters for buying intent without financial friction.
**Price:** Free or $20–$100 paid assessment
**Examples:**
- Free lead magnet (guide, checklist, quiz, report)
- Paid diagnostic assessment
- Free VSL opt-in ("Watch this 45-minute presentation")
- Free webinar registration
- Low-cost workshop ($27–$97)

**What it must do:**
- Identify the avatar's problem with precision
- Deliver immediate value (they leave knowing something they didn't know before)
- Create belief that the root cause has been identified
- Make the next tier the obvious and logical next step

**Agent extraction question:**
> "Right now, what's the first thing someone does when they want to learn more about what you do — is there something free or low-cost they can get or do before they commit to working with you?"

**If nothing exists:** The agent proposes a Tier 1 offer based on the expert's specialty, avatar awareness level, and new vehicle. The most common high-converting format for functional medicine and health experts is a paid assessment ($47–$97) positioned as a diagnostic — *"Find out what's actually driving your [primary symptom]."*

---

### Tier 2 — Core Offer (High-Ticket, Primary Revenue Driver)
**Purpose:** The main transformation. The flagship program. The majority of revenue. The offer the entire funnel exists to sell.
**Price:** $5,000–$8,000+ (clinicIQ standard; brick-and-mortar may vary)
**Fulfillment model:** 1:1, group, hybrid (1:1 coach + group community), or self-paced with coaching support

**The hybrid model (clinicIQ recommended):**
- Dedicated personal coach
- One live check-in per month (Zoom or in-person)
- Ongoing text access via GHL between sessions
- Private community with Open Office Hours
- Each full-time coach carries ~70 clients
- Scales without degrading the transformation promise

**What it must do:**
- Deliver a specific, named transformation in a defined timeframe
- Feel premium enough to justify the price without being inaccessible
- Be deliverable at scale (not 100% dependent on the expert's personal hours)
- Have a clear outcome the avatar can visualize

**Agent extraction questions:**
> "If money was no object and someone was fully committed — what does your highest-level program look like? What do they get, how long does it run, and what's the specific result on the other side?"

> "How many clients are you currently working with at this level, and how are you delivering it?"

> "Is there a way you could serve more people without the result getting worse?"

---

### Tier 3 — Downsell (Accessible Entry for Non-Buyers)
**Purpose:** Capture buyers who want the transformation but can't commit to the core offer price right now. Revenue that would otherwise be lost. Also serves as a proving ground — many downsell buyers convert to Tier 2 after experiencing results.
**Price:** $500–$2,000
**Examples:**
- Self-paced online program with limited coaching access
- 30-day intensive with one coaching call
- Digital protocol or course
- Monthly group coaching at a lower price point

**What it must not be:**
- A consolation prize that makes the buyer feel like a second-class customer
- A watered-down version of the core offer that damages the brand
- Priced so low it creates cognitive dissonance about the core offer's value

**Agent extraction question:**
> "If someone can't afford your main program or isn't ready to commit fully — is there something smaller they can start with that still gets them a real result?"

**If nothing exists:** The agent builds a downsell concept based on what components of the core offer can be delivered asynchronously or in group format at lower cost. Most commonly: a structured self-paced program built around the expert's signature protocol, with one group Q&A call per month.

---

### Tier 4 — Continuity (Monthly Recurring Revenue)
**Purpose:** Keep the relationship alive after the core transformation. Recurring revenue. Retention. Upsell opportunity. Community building. The tier most health experts leave completely on the table.
**Price:** $97–$497/month
**Examples:**
- Monthly supplement subscription (highest margin if the expert has a product line)
- Monthly group coaching or membership
- Annual testing + protocol review program
- Ongoing accountability + community access

**What it must do:**
- Deliver enough ongoing value that cancellation feels like a loss
- Require minimal time from the expert (group formats, automated check-ins)
- Connect naturally to the outcome of the core offer (not a random add-on)

**Agent extraction question:**
> "After someone completes your main program and gets their result — what happens next? Is there anything keeping them in your world, or do they basically graduate and disappear?"

**Why this is the most underbuilt tier:** Most health experts are so focused on the next new client that they never build the system to keep the ones they've already transformed. A 200-person continuity program at $197/month is $39,400/month in recurring revenue that requires almost no additional marketing spend. The agent makes this math explicit.

## The Hormozi Value Equation — Applied to Health Offers

Every offer is scored against Alex Hormozi's value equation. The agent uses this framework both to evaluate the expert's existing offer and to build missing tiers.

**The four value levers:**

### 1. Dream Outcome
What is the specific, vivid result the avatar gets?
- Weak: "Feel better and have more energy"
- Strong: "Get off your thyroid medication, lose the weight that hasn't moved in 5 years, and have the energy you had in your 30s — in 90 days"

The more specific, measurable, and emotionally resonant the dream outcome, the higher the perceived value. The agent works with the expert to make the transformation statement as concrete as possible.

**Extraction question:**
> "When your best patients complete your program — what specifically has changed? Give me the most specific, dramatic version of the result."

### 2. Perceived Likelihood of Achievement
How confident is the avatar that this will work for them specifically?

This is the primary trust variable. It is increased by:
- Social proof (case studies, testimonials, before/afters from similar avatars)
- Credentials and authority signals
- Mechanism clarity (when they understand *why* it works, they believe it will work)
- Guarantee (risk reversal transfers the perceived risk from buyer to seller)
- The specificity of the targeting ("designed for women over 40 with autoimmune thyroid conditions" converts better than "for anyone with thyroid problems")

**Extraction questions:**
> "What results do your patients consistently get? What's a realistic outcome for someone who follows your protocol?"

> "Do you have a guarantee of any kind, or would you be willing to build one? What would you feel comfortable promising?"

### 3. Time to Result
How long until the avatar experiences a meaningful change?

- The shorter the perceived time to first result, the higher the perceived value
- "Quick wins" built into the early program experience are as important as the final outcome
- The agent captures both: time to first noticeable result and time to full transformation

**Extraction question:**
> "How quickly do patients typically start to feel a difference — not full transformation, just the first sign that something is working?"

**Why this matters:** The first result experience is the retention mechanism. When someone feels a difference in week 2, they stay for the full transformation. The Builder Layer uses this field to create "week 2 win" content in email sequences and coaching check-ins.

### 4. Effort and Sacrifice Required
How hard does the avatar have to work to get the result? What do they have to give up?

- The less effort and sacrifice required, the higher the perceived value
- This does not mean the program should be easy — it means the effort should be framed as manageable, supported, and worth it
- The biggest effort objection in health: *"I've tried to change my diet/lifestyle before and I couldn't stick with it."*

**Extraction question:**
> "What does a patient actually have to do to get results in your program? What's required of them day to day?"

**How the agent uses this:** The effort reality gets reframed as "supported effort with a clear path" rather than "hard work." The Builder Layer uses this to write the "What's expected of you" section of the webinar and the fulfillment promise on the order page.

## The Value Stack — Building the Perceived Value Tower

Every high-ticket health offer needs a visible value stack — the itemized list of what the buyer is getting, with individual values assigned to each item, that makes the total perceived value dramatically higher than the price.

The value stack does two things:
1. Makes the price feel small relative to what they're getting
2. Systematically dismantles the "it's too expensive" objection before it can form

**Stack architecture:**

| Element | Description | Assigned Value |
|---------|-------------|----------------|
| Core program | The primary transformation delivery | $X,XXX |
| Personal coach access | Dedicated coach, monthly check-in, text access | $X,XXX |
| Community access | Private group, office hours, peer accountability | $XXX |
| Bonus 1 | Fast-implementation resource (speed) | $XXX |
| Bonus 2 | Belief-shift resource (internal false belief) | $XXX |
| Bonus 3 | External obstacle resource (external false belief) | $XXX |
| Guarantee | Risk reversal — defines what happens if result not achieved | Priceless |
| **Total perceived value** | | **$XX,XXX** |
| **Your investment** | | **$X,XXX** |

**The ratio rule:** Total perceived value should be at least 10x the price. An $8,000 program should have a value stack totaling $80,000+ in perceived value. This is not dishonest — it is an accurate accounting of what it would cost to get these results piecemeal from different sources.

**Agent extraction — bonus ideation:**
> "What additional resources, tools, or access could you offer that would make someone feel like they'd be crazy not to take this deal — things that would help them implement faster, believe more, or remove the most common obstacles?"

The agent generates 5–8 bonus concepts based on:
- The avatar's primary false beliefs (a bonus that directly addresses each false belief)
- Common implementation obstacles (a bonus that removes the most common barrier)
- Quick-win acceleration (a bonus that speeds up the time-to-first-result)
- Social proof amplification (access to a community of others who've done it)

## Offer and Mechanism Naming

One of the highest-leverage activities in this entire section. A named offer and a named mechanism are worth 10x what an unnamed equivalent is worth in the market — because they signal proprietary value and create separation from competition.

### Naming the Mechanism
The new vehicle from Section 5 (Problems → Root Cause → New Vehicle Map) gets a proprietary name here that becomes the brand anchor for everything.

**Good mechanism names:**
- Specific enough to feel different from anything else
- Simple enough to be understood immediately
- Desirable enough to make the avatar want to know more
- Ownable — nobody else has said it this way

**Examples:**
- *"The Cellular Reset Protocol"*
- *"The Autoimmune Root Cause Method"*
- *"The Metabolic Rewiring System"*
- *"The 5-Phase Hormone Restoration Framework"*

**Extraction question:**
> "If you had to give your approach a name — the thing that makes what you do fundamentally different from what anyone else is doing — what would you call it? Or if you haven't named it, what are the core phases or steps?"

**Agent behavior:** If the expert doesn't have a name, the agent generates 3–5 options based on the root cause, the mechanism, the outcome, and the avatar's language. The expert selects or combines.

### Naming the Offer
The high-ticket offer name is different from the mechanism name. The mechanism is what makes it work. The offer name is what they're buying.

**High-converting offer name formula:**
*[Transformation] + [Timeframe] + [Program/System/Method]*

Examples:
- *"The 90-Day Thyroid Restoration Program"*
- *"Metabolic Reset: A 6-Month Transformation System"*
- *"The Autoimmune Recovery Method — 12 Weeks to Root Cause Resolution"*

The name must include a timeframe (creates the Hormozi time-delay score), a specific outcome (dream outcome), and a program descriptor that signals structure and support (reduces effort/sacrifice score).

## The Guarantee Architecture

The guarantee is one of the most underused conversion levers in health marketing. Most practitioners avoid guarantees because they're afraid of chargebacks or giving refunds. The agent reframes this:

**The real purpose of a guarantee:** Transfer the perceived risk from the buyer to the seller. The buyer's biggest fear is spending $6,000–$8,000 and getting nothing. The guarantee says: "I am so confident this works that I'm willing to take on that risk so you don't have to." That confidence is itself a conversion mechanism.

**Health-appropriate guarantee structures:**

| Guarantee Type | Structure | Best Used When |
|---|---|---|
| Results guarantee | "Get [specific result] in [timeframe] or we [work with you until you do / refund you]" | Expert has strong proof bank and high completion rate |
| Effort guarantee | "Do [specific actions] for [timeframe] and if you don't see results, we'll [refund/extend]" | Expert wants to protect against uncommitted buyers |
| Assessment guarantee | "If your paid assessment doesn't give you at least [X value], we'll refund it" | Low-risk entry for the Tier 1 paid assessment |
| Satisfaction guarantee | "If you're not completely satisfied after [30/60 days], we'll give you a full refund" | Broadest risk reversal — highest conversion lift |

**Extraction question:**
> "What are you willing to promise someone who joins your program? What's the most confident claim you can make about what they'll experience — and what would you do if they did everything right and still didn't get the result?"

## Urgency and Scarcity Architecture

Urgency and scarcity are not manipulation tactics. In a health context, they are often true — the practitioner genuinely has limited capacity, limited cohort spots, or a defined enrollment window. The agent extracts real urgency and scarcity levers rather than manufacturing fake ones.

**Legitimate urgency levers in health programs:**
- Cohort-based enrollment (new cohort starts [date], next one in 3 months)
- Limited coaching capacity (coach carries max 70 clients — actual capacity constraint)
- Bonus expiration (fast-action bonuses that genuinely expire at a defined deadline)
- Price increase (current price is introductory; price increases after [date])
- Health urgency (the cost of waiting — "every month you don't address the root cause, [consequence]")

**The health urgency frame:**
The most powerful urgency in health marketing is not artificial scarcity. It is the cost of inaction. The avatar has been waiting to feel better for 3, 5, 10 years. Every month they don't act is another month of lost quality of life. The agent captures what the expert genuinely believes about the cost of delay — and the Builder Layer uses that to build authentic urgency into every close.

**Extraction question:**
> "What do you tell patients who say they want to wait or think about it? What do you know about what happens to people who keep waiting to address this?"

## Pricing Psychology and Price Point History

Two critical fields the agent captures that most funnel builders ignore:

### Price Point History
Has the expert sold at this price level before? What happened?

If yes and it worked: that data becomes proof-of-price in the sales script and de-risks the price point for the Builder Layer.
If yes and it didn't work: the agent diagnoses why (usually value presentation, not the price itself) and adjusts the value stack and offer framing accordingly.
If no, it's new: the agent flags this and suggests a lower-risk launch mechanism (beta cohort at a discounted price to build proof, then full price on second cohort).

**Field populated:** `offer.price_point_history`

### Income Level Alignment
The avatar's income level (captured in Section 4) is cross-referenced against the offer price. The agent flags any misalignment and proposes adjustments to either the price, the payment plan structure, or the Tier 3 downsell positioning.

**Payment plan architecture:**
Most high-ticket health offers convert better with a payment plan option. Common structures:
- 3 payments of $X (total slightly higher than single pay — typically 15–20% premium)
- 6 payments of $X (makes the monthly cost feel accessible vs. the lump sum)
- Single pay with a meaningful discount (rewards commitment and improves cash flow)

## Silent Diagnosis — Offer Gaps Flagged During Extraction

While the Onboarding Agent captures offer architecture, it is simultaneously running a silent diagnostic against the clinicIQ 4-tier standard. Any gap is flagged in the dossier for the Strategy Layer to address.

**Common offer gap patterns and their diagnoses:**

| Gap Pattern | Diagnosis | Strategy Layer Response |
|---|---|---|
| No Tier 1 attraction offer | No lead generation mechanism | Build lead magnet or paid assessment as first asset |
| Core offer under $3,000 | Underpriced for transformation delivered | Reframe value stack, rebuild offer name, reprice with payment plan |
| No downsell | Revenue leak — losing buyers who want it but can't commit | Build digital program or group option |
| No continuity offer | Churn after core transformation — revenue reset each month | Build membership or subscription structure |
| Core offer is 100% 1:1 with expert | Not scalable — revenue ceiling = expert's hours | Hybrid model redesign |
| Offer name is generic | No differentiation — competing on price by default | Mechanism naming session |
| No guarantee | Risk barrier reducing conversion | Guarantee architecture consultation |
| No value stack | Price feels like a number not a value | Full value stack build |

Every flagged gap becomes a priority item in the Growth Strategy Agent's 90-day plan.
